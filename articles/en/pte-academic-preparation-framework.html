PTE Academic Preparation Framework

PTE Academic is a computer-based English proficiency test covering speaking, writing, reading, and listening. Preparation can be organized around task structure, scoring traits, oral stability, writing-form control, and audio-text information processing.

Test Structure

The official test format is divided into Speaking & Writing, Reading, and Listening. Reading contains five question types. Listening contains eight question types, based on audio or video clips that usually play once. Scores are reported on a 10–90 scale and mapped to CEFR levels.

PartCore abilityStructural feature
Speaking & WritingSpoken production, pronunciation, fluency, written organizationSeveral tasks use partial-credit scoring; irrelevant, missing, or copied responses may receive no credit.
ReadingSemantic understanding, grammatical cues, paragraph logicIncludes dropdown blanks, multiple choice, reorder paragraph, drag-and-drop blanks, and single answer.
ListeningListening discrimination, note taking, summarization, spellingIncludes spoken summary, blanks, incorrect-word highlighting, and dictation tasks.

Scoring Traits

Many PTE tasks are integrated-skill tasks rather than single-skill exercises. Speaking tasks commonly involve Content, Pronunciation, and Oral Fluency. Writing and dictation tasks also involve traits such as Form, Grammar, Vocabulary, and Spelling. A useful preparation model separates information completeness, format compliance, and output stability.

A general preparation weighting can be summarized as: high-frequency integrated tasks first, low-tolerance format tasks next, and pure selection tasks after that. This is an organizational model, not an official scoring table.

Speaking Tasks

The core of speaking preparation is not complex expression, but a stable speech stream. Read Aloud and Repeat Sentence are sensitive to order, completeness, and fluency. Describe Image and Retell Lecture require continuous output from limited information. Templates provide syntactic scaffolding rather than content replacement.

TraitTraining objectObservable indicator
FluencySpeed, pausing, continuityFew long pauses, few self-corrections, even speech flow
PronunciationVowels, consonants, stress, connected speechCommon words are recognized consistently; final sounds are not systematically dropped
ContentKeyword and topic coverageMain information from the image, lecture, or sentence is retained

Writing Tasks

Summarize Written Text has strong formal constraints, usually requiring the central meaning to be compressed into one sentence with controlled word count and punctuation. Essay writing depends more on paragraph organization, stance, development, connectors, and grammatical accuracy. Writing templates function as structural tools; excessive template dependence increases the risk of irrelevant content and rigid expression.

Reading and Listening Tasks

Reading fill-in tasks rely on context, part of speech, and collocation. Reorder Paragraph relies on topic sentences, reference chains, time sequence, and logical connectors. Listening tasks are constrained by one-pass audio, so note taking needs to support answer production rather than full transcription.

Write from Dictation requires sentence meaning, keywords, and function words to be retained together. Highlight Incorrect Words requires synchronous matching between audio input and screen text; attention lag substantially increases mistaken selections.

Neutral Template Frames

Describe Image

The image presents information about [topic]. The main feature is [largest / most visible item]. Another noticeable feature is [secondary item]. The figure also shows [contrast / trend / smallest item]. Overall, the image provides a clear overview of [topic].

Retell Lecture

The lecture discusses [topic]. First, the speaker mentions [key point 1]. Second, the speaker explains [key point 2]. In addition, the lecture refers to [example or supporting detail]. Overall, the lecture focuses on [central idea].

Summarize Spoken Text

The recording mainly discusses [topic]. It explains [main point], and also mentions [supporting point 1] and [supporting point 2]. The speaker further refers to [example or evidence]. Overall, the recording highlights [central conclusion].

Essay

The topic raises a debate about [issue]. One position emphasizes [view A], while another position focuses on [view B]. This essay argues that [main position] because of [reason 1] and [reason 2]. The first reason is [reason 1]. For example, [example]. This indicates that [connection to argument]. Another factor is [reason 2]. In social, academic, or professional contexts, [explanation]. This supports the view that [connection to argument]. In conclusion, [main position] is supported by [reason 1] and [reason 2].
